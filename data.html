From Spreadsheets to Data Strategy

My relationship with data didn't start with a course. It started with a problem — a renewal and sales tracker at Pond Insurance Agency that was being calculated manually every month, quietly accumulating error risk that nobody had flagged. I built a formula-driven Excel dashboard with automated calculations and visual summaries. Management adopted it for quarterly and annual reporting. That's still in active use today.

That's what I mean by practical data work. Not visualisations for their own sake — tools that solve real operational problems, reduce manual error, and give decision-makers cleaner information faster.

Currently I am completing the Google Data Analytics Certificate on Coursera — a structured program covering data cleaning, analysis, visualisation, SQL fundamentals, R basics, and the full analytical workflow from question to insight. This is the formal foundation that contextualises the applied work I've already been doing.

After the Google certificate, the roadmap continues with Power BI — because the ability to build interactive dashboards that non-technical stakeholders can actually use is one of the most transferable skills in any operations or management role. Salesforce Trailhead follows, specifically to substantiate existing resume claims and deepen CRM data literacy. Python basics run throughout as a thread — initially for data manipulation and scripting, eventually connecting into the cybersecurity and AI work happening in parallel.

The longer-term target for this section is to document the full progression from spreadsheet-level data work to analytics tooling to data-informed operations strategy. Insurance is a data-dense industry — policy records, premium calculations, claims history, renewal pipelines. The operational experience I have in that environment is directly transferable to data analyst roles that sit inside insurance, fintech, or any compliance-adjacent organisation.

When this page is complete, it will contain project writeups, dashboard samples, certificate documentation, and annotated analysis work that demonstrates not just tool proficiency but analytical thinking — the difference between someone who can use Excel and someone who knows what question to ask before opening it.
